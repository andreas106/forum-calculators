About the Sports Futures Calculator

A futures bet is a wager on an outcome that won't be decided until far in the future — who wins the Super Bowl, the NBA MVP, the Premier League title, the F1 Constructors' Championship, a major golf tournament. Sportsbooks list every contender with long odds (often +400 to +50000), and the bet settles only when the season or tournament ends.

This calculator shows what a winning futures bet pays. Enter your stake and the offered odds in any common format, and you'll see total payout, net profit, and the implied win probability the book is pricing in. Use it before placing a futures ticket to size your stake or compare prices across books.

Why futures markets carry the heaviest vig

Add up the implied probabilities of every runner in a futures market and the total usually exceeds 120-140%. On a sportsbook's NBA championship market with 30 teams, the implied probabilities might sum to 130%, meaning the book's hold is around 23%. That's three to four times the hold on a standard point-spread market. The reason: long resolution times, lower volume, and books needing margin against the small chance a wild outcome cleans them out. The math here doesn't fix that — you still pay the vig — but knowing the implied probability lets you spot when a runner is mispriced relative to your own model.

The other thing to know about futures: dead money. Your stake is locked at the book from the moment you place the bet until the market resolves. A +800 NFL Super Bowl ticket placed in September won't pay out until February. The opportunity cost of that capital matters if you bet large — a 6-month hold at 5% is a 2.5% cost on the stake, which eats into expected value.

Hedging a futures ticket

A futures ticket whose runner reaches the final stage of a tournament can be hedged on the final match to lock in profit. For example, an early-season +2500 ticket on Kansas City to win the Super Bowl becomes much shorter once they reach the Super Bowl — hedging by betting the other team gives you a guaranteed return regardless of the result. Use this calculator to compute the futures payout, then a parlay or arbitrage calculator to size the hedge.

Odds format reference

American (+800 / −150)
Standard US format. Long futures prices are almost always quoted as positive numbers in the hundreds or thousands.
Decimal (9.00)
Total return per 1 unit staked. Multiply stake × decimal for total payout. Easier than American for long prices.
Fractional (8/1)
Net profit per unit staked. 8/1 means risk 1 to win 8. Common in horse racing and UK futures markets.
Hong Kong (8.00)
Net profit per 1 unit staked, stake excluded. Equals decimal odds minus 1.
Indonesian (8.00)
American odds divided by 100. Common across Asian sportsbooks.
Malay (−0.13)
Capped between −1 and 1. Negative for short-priced favourites, positive for underdogs. Long futures prices typically show as small negative numbers.
Implied probability (11.11%)
The break-even win rate at the offered odds. If your true estimate beats the implied number, the ticket has positive expected value before vig adjustments.
